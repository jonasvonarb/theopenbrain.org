: Refraction Errors: Despite our body’s best laid plans, a variety of developmental and age-related issues can cause our eyes to improperly focus incoming images onto the retina, resulting in impaired vision. Two common issues result from the image being focused either in front of (myopia) or behind (hyperopia) the retina. Myopia, also commonly referred to as nearsightedness, blurs images of far-away images. In contrast, hyperopia, commonly referred to as farsightedness, blurs images of nearby images. Another common refractive issue is astigmatism, which results in uneven focus of the incoming image on the retina, due to irregular curvature of the cornea or lens abnormalities. Finally, as we age, the lens becomes less elastic and the ciliary muscles become less effective at accommodating different focal planes, leading to presbyopia, which tends to focus images behind the retina. In general, most types of refractive errors can be addressed with corrective lenses.

2.3 Retinal cell classes and types

For the purposes of this chapter, we define classes as a high-level category, and retinal neurons are split into 5: photoreceptors, horizontal cells, bipolar cells, amacrine cells, ganglion cells. In turn, we define types as a low-level category, as each class can be split into a number of cell types (e.g. different types of photoreceptors). Within a given class of retinal neurons, different cell types serve unique functional roles. Evidence for this division of cell classes into subtypes was evident from the earliest anatomical studies on the retina: for instance, drawings of photoreceptors by the anatomist Max Schultz showed cells with two distinct morphologies, revealing rods and cones1. More recent work has shown that distinct retinal cell types can be distinguished from one another by gene expression profiles, connectivity patterns, morphology and functional response properties17–27. Furthermore, for a given cell type there appears to be a strong correlation between each of these metrics. Below, we briefly introduce the different retinal classes and discuss them in greater detail later in the chapter.

2.3.1 Photoreceptors: Photoreceptors are vital to retinal function, as they detect light and convert it into an electrical signal28,29. By the late 17th century, it had become clear there were 2 types of photoreceptors, rods and cones, and that each was differentially localized in the retina and served different functions. Anatomical work from Max Schultze indicated that cones were mostly located near the center of the human retina, whereas rods dominated in the periphery1. Behavioral work from Hermann Aubert showed that visual acuity and colour discrimination both decreased from the center to the periphery of the visual field1. 

 

2.3.2 Bipolar Cells: Bipolar cell somata occupy the middle region of the inner nuclear layer and are named for their characteristic anatomy: elongated cells with dendrites in the outer plexiform layer where they collect glutamatergic signals from photoreceptors, and axons in the inner plexiform layer where they relay photoreceptor signals to amacrine and ganglion cells 6,10,29. 

 

2.3.3 Horizontal cells: Horizontal cells modulate signals passing between photoreceptors and bipolar cells via feedback to photoreceptors (and feedforward signals to bipolar cells). While these were the first retinal neurons whose light responses were measured with intracellular recording — by Gunner Svaetichin in the 1950s — many details about how they contribute to retinal processing remain unresolved. 

 

2.3.4 Amacrine Cells: The most diverse set of cells in the retina are amacrine cells17,21. These are, with a few exceptions, inhibitory interneurons which collect input from bipolar cells and in turn inhibit bipolar, ganglion and other amacrine cells. In some vertebrate species, amacrine cells comprise more than 50 different cell types, each exhibiting a unique anatomy, physiology, and gene expression profile5. While some amacrine cells exhibit distinct dendrites and an axon, many have processes that act as both pre and postsynaptic elements. Attempts to generalize are difficult in the face of this diversity, however some trends have been observed: first, almost all amacrine cells are inhibitory neurons releasing either GABA or glycine onto their targets; Second, glycinergic amacrine cells tend to have narrow dendritic trees, and thus small receptive fields, whereas GABAergic cells tend to have broader dendritic trees and thus larger receptive fields30; Third, many amacrine cells release an additional neurotransmitter, which can be excitatory, but is more commonly a neuromodulator such as a peptide or catecholamine5,6.

2.3.5 Retinal Ganglion Cells: Ganglion cells are the only retinal neurons that send axons out of the eye to the rest of the brain. In addition, with a few exceptions — some amacrine cells, and possibly some bipolar cells — they are the only retinal neurons that fire action potentials. They generate these signals in response to the combination of excitatory and inhibitory inputs they receive from bipolar and amacrine cells, respectively. As discussed in detail below, there are several types of retinal ganglion cells5,17, and each is specialized to detect specific aspects of the visual scene, such as colours, edges and movement.

2.4 Synaptic architecture 

Neurons in the retina are highly interconnected through a web of both chemical and electrical synapse. Below, we briefly summarize the general synaptic connectivity principles in the retina.

2.4.1 Chemical synapses. Photoreceptors form glutamatergic synapses with bipolar and horizontal cells, and bipolar cells form glutamatergic synapses with amacrine and ganglion cells. Amacrine cells form inhibitory GABAergic and glycinergic synapses with bipolar ganglion and amacrine cells6,28,29. Ganglion cell axons make glutamatergic synapses with retinorecipient targets throughout the brain31. 

 

Neurotransmitter release in photoreceptors and bipolar cells occurs at specialized presynaptic structures called synaptic ribbons[STD2] , which permit fast and continuous vesicular release from these neurons that signal via graded potentials32. Each active zone within a photoreceptor or bipolar cell axon terminal possesses a ribbon shaped organelle which is attached to the presynaptic membrane at its edges, and tethers synaptic vesicles along its surface33. The ribbon allows for a ~5-10-fold increase in the number of vesicles that can be docked at the active zone as compared to a conventional synapse32,33. In response to depolarization and calcium influx into the axon terminal, vesicles move down the face of the ribbon like on a conveyor belt, being shuttled to the release site. 

2.4.2 Electrical synapses. Electrical synapses are widespread in the retina34. Also known as gap junctions, these connections form between retinal neurons bearing matched connexin proteins, which form channels that connects the intracellular space of neighbouring cells. These synapses, unlike chemical synapses, allow electrical signals to pass reciprocally between coupled neighbours. Gap junctions play various functional roles, from guiding rod signals throughout the retina, to synchronizing signals between coupled neurons, to strengthening the lateral flow of signals through retinal circuits34. 

  1. Photoreceptors and phototransduction

Photoreceptors are the most evolutionary ancient part of the retina, and likely evolved from ciliated sensory cells that blanketed the skin of our early submarine ancestors35–38. They are long thin cells with a soma situated in the outer nuclear layer, a nerve terminal located in the outer plexiform layer, and an elongated outer segment with opsin-filled disks extending towards the retina pigment epithelium. Each outer segment contains roughly 1000 membranous disks arranged like a stack of coins. Each disk is densely packed with molecules of photopigment-filled opsins and their associated phototransduction machinery. This disc organization is critical for photoreceptor function because it amplifies photoreceptor surface area by ~1000-fold, greatly increasing the number of light-catching opsin molecules within a single photoreceptor39. A thin stalk connects the opsin-filled outer segment with the mitochondria-rich inner segment, which helps sustain the tremendous energy costs of phototransduction10,29. Vertebrate photoreceptors can be morphologically divided into 2 classes based on the shape of their outer segments. Cone photoreceptors exhibit tapered outer segments (i.e. their outer segment is shaped like a cone), are specialized to detect photons during daylight conditions, and possess various opsin variants that make them sensitive to photons of different wavelengths. Humans have 3 cone types, with maximal photon absorptions situated in the blue (peak excitation ~420 nm), green (peak excitation ~534 nm) and red (peak excitation ~564 nm) regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. In contrast to cones, rods have long (rod-shaped) outer segments, and are specialized for detecting photons in dim environments10,29. Discs in rod outer segments contain rhodopsin molecules attuned to wavelengths in the center of the visible range (blue-green; peak excitation ~498 nm). Rhodopsin is more densely concentrated in rod outer segments than cone-opsins are in cone outer segments. Additionally, in rods, the disks in the outer segment are wholly internal to the cell’s outer membrane, whereas in cones the outer membrane invaginates multiple times in the outer segment and itself contains the opsin molecules. The disks have a lifespan of roughly 12 days, after which time they are shed at the outer segment’s tip and phagocytosed by the surrounding retinal pigment epithelium. In turn, new disks are added at the base of the outer segment.

3.1 Phototransduction

Stars, like our sun, emit energy along various parts of the electromagnetic (EM) spectrum. In fact, most of the photons we encounter in the world are outside our visible range — the vast majority of these are in the infrared: they create a warm sensation when absorbed by our skin, but we cannot see them. Visible photons occupy a narrow band of the EM spectrum, and interestingly, are among a subset of photons that best penetrate sea water – where a large family of opsins evolved over millions of years35,36. Below, we discuss how these ‘visible’ photons are detected by photoreceptors in the retina and get converted into electrical signals. 

The eye and retina act as a camera and film, designed to collect incoming photons. Catching photons is hard since they move fast and because so many are lost on the trip from the surface of the eye to the retina. To illustrate this point, we describe a thought experiment initially put forward by Bob Rodieck10: Imagine you are camping, peering up at the North Star in the night sky. The star’s brightness at the corneal surface is ~2800 photons/sec/mm2, and like a windowpane the cornea transmits most of this light, though some (~2.5%) is reflected away. About 9% of the photons that pass through the cornea are absorbed by the anterior compartment, and the remainder (2350 photons/sec/mm2) reach the pupil. Since it is a very dark night, your pupil is fully dilated (~40mm2), allowing roughly 94,000 visible photons to pass through the iris and lens every second. More than half (~57%) of these 94,000 photons are absorbed by the vitreous humour before arriving at the retina. Due to ongoing eye movements, different small patches of retina see the starlight every 100ms or so. In this short time window, ~1100 photons strike a given retinal patch containing ~120 photoreceptors, meaning that ~9 photons enter each photoreceptor. Converting this weak signal into the clear perception of the North Star is the job of the phototransduction machinery.28

 

3.1.1 Rhodopsin.[LLM3]  In the mid-1870s, Franz Boll identified a substance in the frog retina that bleached when exposed to light — it became known as “visual purple” or rhodopsin and is the light sensing protein in rod photoreceptors40,41. Rhodopsin contains the opsin retinal, which is synthesized from vitamin A29,41. Even as far back as ancient Egypt, the Ebers medical papyrus dating from ~1500 BCE recommended treating night blindness with liver (which happens to be high in vitamin A, though the ancient Egyptians recommended topically applying liquid from the liver directly to the eye)1. The bleaching that Boll had observed was the result of retinal undergoing a photoisomerization from 11-cis to all-trans retinal. This isomerization triggers conformational changes in rhodopsin, which initiates a signal transduction cascade. Subtle amino-acid variations in the structure of the opsin change the way 11-cis retinal is ‘held’ within the rhodopsin molecule, and alter the photon wavelength required for a photoisomerzation event29,41,42. This is a core difference between opsins that detect blue, green, and red photons (such as those in cones). The conversion is remarkably efficient, with approximately 2/3 of absorbed photons resulting in a photoisomerization41. By comparison, this quantum efficiency is only 1/10 for photographic film.

 

Full width section: Phototransduction. In the dark, the photoreceptor outer segment maintains a high concentration of the small molecule cGMP (cyclic guanosine monophosphate), which tonically activates cGMP-gated cationic channels. This results in Na+ and Ca2+ inflow in the outer segment, which balances an ongoing efflux of K+ ions in the inner segment. This ionic flux results in a relatively depolarized resting membrane potential (Vm) of ~-30mV in darkness. Rhodopsin is a G-protein coupled receptor. It becomes enzymatically active following photon absorption and in turn activates the G-protein transducin. Transducin is composed of a, b, and g subunits, and upon activation, releases its bg subunit. Its a subunit in turn activates a phosphodiesterase (PDE) that then hydrolyzes cGMP molecules. The resulting decrease in intracellular [cGMP] in the outer segment leads to the closure of cGMP-gated cation channels28. Since the efflux of K+ ions continues in the inner segment, closing cGMP-gated channels in the outer segment results in photoreceptor membrane hyperpolarization, leading to a decrease in glutamate release. This light-evoked hyperpolarization is called a photovoltage, and its amplitude and duration depend on the luminance, size and duration of the light flash. Finally, an activated rhodopsin molecule needs to be de-activated, otherwise the phototransduction cascade would continue endlessly. Phototransduction terminates when an activated rhodopsin is phosphorylated by a membrane-bound enzyme called rhodopsin kinase. The addition of a phosphate to rhodopsin leads to its binding by a protein called arrestin, which ejects the rhodopsin kinase, and quenches rhodopsin’s ability to activate transducin by breaking the bond between all-trans retinal and opsin28,29,42.

3.1.2 Counting photons: The photoreceptor is given a difficult task. In a world of ever-changing light levels, the photoreceptor needs to detect photons, and convert them into an electrical signal that regulates its release of glutamate. Complicating this task, the driving force through the cGMP-gated channels changes as the photoreceptors membrane potential dynamically depolarizes and hyperpolarizes. This might be expected to lead to different photoresponses to an equivalent visual stimulus depending on the history of what the photoreceptor just saw. Fortunately, the photoreceptor can effectively ‘count photons’, without strongly being impacted by previous photons. How? Plotting the current that flows through a rod cGMP-gated channel across different membrane voltages reveals a strong rectification: photocurrents are relatively fixed across negative membrane potentials (where the photoreceptor generally operates)28. This rectification helps make the photovoltage proportional to the flash intensity that evoked it, rather than also being dependent on the history of prior flashes. 

[STD4] 

3.1.3 Signal and noise. cGMP-channels are abundant in photoreceptors28. Each can drive a very small current, about 3fA.  This single channel current is extremely small (most channels pass currents about 3 orders of magnitude greater). Why not use a larger single channel current for signaling photon absorption? If we consider signal and noise, it becomes clear why small currents are better in this situation. Channel opening is subject to thermodynamic noise. As a consequence, cGMP-channels in photoreceptors are frequently flickering between open and closed states. If the single channel current was very large, these flickering events could lead to large variations in the cell’s membrane potential, which could incorrectly be interpreted by the organism as photons that were detected. Thus, the retina’s strategy divides ‘thermal noise’ across many channels, each with very small currents. The noise of different channels is independent, and thus is averaged out. In contrast, the signal (i.e. the detection of photons produced by a flash of light) is synchronized across channels and photoreceptors, and is averaged in. 

3.1.4: Signal amplification and single photon detection: A single photon hitting a rhodopsin molecule causes it to get activated for around 100 ms. During this time, a significant intracellular amplification cascade takes place. First, the activated rhodopsin activates ~700 transducin molecules, which in turn activate ~700 phosphodiesterase molecules (in other words, each PDE activates 1 transducin). This results in the degradation of ~1400 cGMP molecules, leading to the closure of ~230 cGMP-gated channels (~2% of total cGMP-gated channels), and resulting in a membrane hyperpolarization of about 1 mV28.

 

Full width section: The Visual Cycle: When rhodopsin absorbs a photon, its light-catching 11-cis retinal chromophore is isomerized into all-trans retinal. This latter form cannot be used for photon absorption, and the retina undertakes a complex series of steps to convert all-trans retinal back to photo-activatable 11-cis retinal. Following binding of arrestin, the bond between all-trans retinal and the opsin is broken. The all-trans aldehyde is released and quickly converted to all-trans retinol by membrane bound retinal dehydrogenase, which is in turn bound by an all-trans retinol binding protein. From here, a transport protein called interphotoreceptor retinoid-binding protein (IRBP) takes all-trans retinol on an inter-cellular journey into the retina pigment epithelium, which intercalates with photoreceptor outer segments. The retina pigment epithelium contains a pair of enzymes — retinyl-ester isomerase which converts all-trans retinol to 11-cis retinol, and 11-cis retinol dehydrogenase which uses ATP to convert 11-cis retinol back into 11-cis retinal. The regenerated 11-cis retinal is highly insoluble and is quickly bound by an IRBP which encapsulates the molecule, which makes the return trip out of retina pigment epithelium and back into the photoreceptor outer segment, where it is released and binds anew with an opsin28,41.

 

3.1.5 Light sensitivity and temporal differences between rods and cones. Rods are more sensitive to photons than cones28,43. Part of the reason lies in their relatively greater opsin content compared to their cone counterparts28,29. Another part of the answer lies in different isoforms of the phototransduction machinery43. Briefly, the phototransduction isoforms expressed in rods lead them to break down cGMP more rapidly, leading to a higher photon response gain in rods than in cones43. Another part of the answer comes from lower levels of GTPase‐accelerating proteins in rods – these proteins increase the rate at which transducin hydrolyses GTP and becomes inactive. These differences permit rods to detect a greater number of photons (higher opsin concentration), turn on transduction machinery more quickly, and stay active for longer. 

The increased sensitivity of rods incurs two principal costs: responsiveness and bleaching. Because rods are so much more sensitive than cones (up to 1000x in the human retina), it also means that quick changes in illumination are likely to be ‘smoothed’ out44. Rods also tend to run out of 11-cis retinal more quickly than their cone counterparts under sustained illumination, which prevents them from sensing any additional photons. However, this difference also occurs because cones possess an intrinsic ability to isomerize all-trans retinal back into its light-sensing 11-cis isoform.

  1. Horizontal cells modulate signaling between photoreceptors and bipolar cells

The visual world contains a tremendous amount of redundancy. Take a look at the room you are sitting in while you read this text, or look at the border between the land and sky if you happen to be outside or near a window. Encoding any portion of the visual world is costly to the brain, so it is advantageous to limit the encoding of redundant parts of the visual world. A key solution our brains use is to focus processing power on local differences in the incoming image, such as edges or local motion. This process occurs in two ways: first, ethologically important features are specifically encoded by the visual system (this is discussed in detail in Section 6 below); second, information processing is actively minimized in parts of the visual field that show high redundancy, via center-surround mechanisms.  Center-surround antagonism begins at the first synapse of the visual pathway, where it is implemented by horizontal cells. Each horizontal cell contacts many photoreceptors with its dendrites and/or axons, and responds to photoreceptor glutamate release via ionotropic glutamate receptors in its dendrites. Paired recordings from photoreceptors and horizontal cells revealed that injecting hyperpolarizing current into a horizontal cell causes a depolarization in the photoreceptor45. 

Glutamate released from a single photoreceptor has a very small effect on a horizontal cell’s membrane potential, whereas when multiple neighbouring photoreceptors signal in tandem, this has a significant effect on the horizontal cell. Furthermore, horizontal cells are densely interconnected with one another via gap junctions, enabling them to efficiently pool signals when a visual stimulus is large and relatively homogeneous. As such, a small visual stimulus that only activates a small number of cones, such as a star in the sky, evokes little horizontal cell feedback. In contrast, a large visual stimulus that activates many adjacent cones, such as a cloudless blue sky, evokes significant horizontal cell feedback. While this surround feedback effect is well described, its mechanism remains controversial, and may rely on a combination of chemical synapses, ephaptic and pH related mechanisms. 

  1. Bipolar cells diversify photoreceptor generated light responses

Bipolar cells receive signals from photoreceptors and deliver them to neurons of the inner retina. While photoreceptors all respond similarly when activated by a photon of light (i.e. they hyperpolarize), bipolar cells come in a variety of different types, which allow distinct bipolar cells to respond differentially to the same signal emanating from photoreceptors. In primates, midget bipolar cells are thought to synapse with individual photoreceptors in the fovea and relay their signals onto individual retinal ganglion cells — thus maintaining a high-resolution channel through the retina7. Bipolar cells outside this foveal region are thought to connect with increasing numbers of photoreceptors at greater eccentricities from the fovea7. Each bipolar cell extends an axon into the inner plexiform layer6. Bipolar cells can be subdivided based on their gene expression profile20,46, whether they depolarize to increases or decreases in luminance, and how their responses vary to visual stimuli flickered at various temporal frequencies26. In the primate retina, there appear to be roughly 10 types of bipolar cells47. 

OFF bipolar cells ‘sign-conserve’ (copy) the photoreceptor signal. Photoreceptor axon terminals release glutamate onto their targets with the help of synaptic ribbon machinery. In an environment of stable luminance, this machinery permits photoreceptors to tonically release glutamate into the synaptic cleft, and enables the photoreceptor to encode both increases in luminance (resulting in membrane hyperpolarization and a decrease in glutamate release), as well as decreases in luminance (resulting in membrane depolarization and an increase in glutamate release). OFF bipolar cell dendrites respond to photoreceptor-released glutamate with ionotropic AMPA and Kainate type glutamate receptors. These receptors are non-selective cation channels that pass inward currents upon binding glutamate. Thus, OFF bipolar cells hyperpolarize to a flash of light and depolarize following a decrease in luminance (responding similarly to the photoreceptors, in a ‘sign-conserving’ manner). OFF bipolar cell axons make synapses with amacrine and ganglion cells in the half of the inner plexiform layer, closest to the inner nuclear layer.

 

ON bipolar cells ‘sign-invert’ the photoreceptor signal. Unlike OFF bipolar cells, which possess ionotropic glutamate receptors in their dendrites, ON bipolar cell dendrites express a unique metabotropic glutamate receptor: mGluR648,49. When bound to glutamate, these receptors activate an intracellular G protein pathway, leading to the closure of a non-selective cation channel — TRPM129,50. Thus, when photoreceptors are releasing high levels of glutamate in the dark, glutamate binds to mGluR6 and closes TRPM1 channels, which hyperpolarizes ON bipolar cells. Increments in light intensity lower glutamate levels in the synaptic cleft, which releases the inhibition of TRPM1 by mGluR6, driving a depolarizing inward current, thereby activating ON bipolar cells. As such, ON bipolar cells are described as being able to ‘sign-invert’ the photoreceptor light response (i.e. when photoreceptors hyperpolarize, ON bipolar cells depolarize). Mutations within the G protein pathway in ON bipolar cells can result in congenital stationary night blindness, because rod signals are passed through a type of ON bipolar cell called a rod bipolar cell (described in more detail in Section 6 below). ON bipolar cell axons make synapses with amacrine and ganglion cells in the half of the inner plexiform layer, closest to the ganglion cell layer. Much of the pioneering work, showing different axonal lamination patterns for ON and OFF channels in the retina, was led by Helga Kolb.

Bipolar cells encode luminance, not patterns. The synaptic ribbons employed at photoreceptor-to-bipolar cell synapses lead to a relatively linear relationship between stimulus intensity and glutamate release, resulting in linear bipolar cell responses51. Most bipolar cells (at least those outside of the fovea), receive input from multiple photoreceptors, and within their receptive field center integrate inputs linearly across space. For example, if we imagine that each bipolar cell center sums the input from 10 photoreceptors, then a light pattern falling on the bipolar cell receptive field that darkens 5 of these photoreceptors from a baseline level (glutamate increase) and brightens the other 5 (glutamate decrease), does not lead to a change in bipolar cell membrane potential, as globally its dendrites do not experience a change in glutamate concentration. Linear responses at this level of the retinal circuit mean that bipolar cells signal the mean luminance over all their connected photoreceptors, rather than encoding the spatial organization of the incoming stimulus. However, in reality things are a bit more complicated, as like photoreceptors, bipolar cells also have inhibitory receptive field surrounds, meaning that what is presented outside their receptive field center can modulate their response to what is shown in their receptive field center.

Bipolar cells temporally filter the visual signal. Aside from splitting the visual world into ON and OFF components, bipolar cells also sort temporal aspects of the incoming visual scene52,53. For instance, some bipolar cell types respond transiently to an abrupt change in light intensity and are optimized for encoding fast changes in luminance over there receptive fields, whereas others respond in a sustained manner to changes in light intensity and are optimized to encode the mean luminance over their receptive fields. These different ‘interpretations’ of the incoming stimulus appear to be partly cell-intrinsic, relying on different synaptic receptors and membrane channels. The kinetically distinct bipolar cell signals can be used by downstream amacrine and ganglion cells for feature-detecting computations, as outlined in Section 6.

[STD5]  

Bipolar cell axon terminals rectify the visual signal. Much like photoreceptors, bipolar cell axon terminals possess synaptic ribbons that help with continuous neurotransmitter release. However, unlike photoreceptor terminals which release glutamate in a manner that is linearly related to stimulus intensity, many bipolar cell ‘rectify’ the luminance signal54 (i.e. they regulate the amount of glutamate released mainly in response to either light increments (in the case of ON bipolar cells) or light decrements (in the case of OFF bipolar cells)). For example, the change in glutamate release from an ON bipolar cell is far greater for a stimulus that is twice as bright as the background intensity than for a stimulus that is half as dim as the background intensity. The opposite is true for OFF bipolar cells. Such rectified bipolar cell output helps downstream ganglion cells become sensitive to patterns within their receptive fields.

  1. Amacrine and Ganglion Cells: retinal circuits, computations and output

The parcellation of photoreceptor-generated signals into distinct visual channels culminates at the ganglion cell. The earliest physiological recordings from the optic nerve — by Rachel Matthews and Edgar Adrian in the 1920s55 — revealed that retinal ganglion send signals to the brain as volleys of action potentials. The retina sends out many different channels of visual content to the brain, each carrying distinct information about the incoming visual scene, with some channels focused on motion, others on colour, others on size, etc17,31. It appears that roughly 15 visual channels leave the primate retina56. These channels are represented by different types of ganglion cells6,17,46. While ganglion cells are the focus of this section, their response properties are intertwined with the amacrine cells they are connected to, so here we discuss both cell types in combination. Furthermore, while some amacrine cells fire action potentials, and some bipolar cells can generate spike-like events, ganglion cells are generally considered the predominant spiking cell in the retina. Another important property of ganglion cells is that many of them can independently relay inputs that arise in a portion of their receptive field — a remarkable finding by Christina Enroth-Cugell and John Robson. In the bipolar cell section, we outlined how bipolar cells are unable to distinguish between a grey background and a stimulus that half-illuminates and half-darkens their receptive field. It turns out that some ganglion cells can respond to such stimuli, encoding both ON and OFF inputs. Such processing in ganglion cells is referred to as a non-linearity 57,58. This property enhances a ganglion cell’s ability to encode patterns within its receptive field. Below, we outline several canonical circuit computations in the retina, which result in ganglion cells with distinct feature selectivities, each likely serving specific ethologically-relevant functions.

6.1 Circuit computations

6.1.1 Center-surround: Recording from ganglion cell axons in the horseshoe crab in the 1930s, Haldan Hartline found that retinal ganglion cells had receptive fields — areas over which a visual stimulus had to be localized in order to activate the cell59. He also found that different ganglion cells could respond either to increments (ON) or decrements (OFF) of light intensity (or both, ON-OFF). Subsequently, in the 1950s Horace Barlow and Steven Kuffler found that ganglion cell receptive fields could be divided into 2 parts: the center and the surround60,61. In these studies, a spot of light was placed at several positions relative to a ganglion cell’s soma. At positions within a circular region close to the cell body (the center), the spot caused the neuron to fire. Spots placed in position beyond this center region (the surround) caused no firing (or even a decrease from the baseline firing rate). This surrounding region was also somewhat circular in shape and placing a spot in this region simultaneously with a spot in the center produced less firing than when a spot was placed in the center alone. Similar results were obtained for both ON and OFF types of ganglion cells. 

Work over the following decades would reveal the circuitry underlying center and surround mechanisms. Center responses are driven by excitatory bipolar cell input which is collected over a ganglion cell’s dendritic tree. Surround responses are driven predominantly by horizontal cells providing surround inhibition in the outer retina, and by GABAergic amacrine cells that reside (laterally) further away and relay visual signals to the central ganglion cell. Center bipolar cells, and the bipolar cells that activate amacrine in the surround, are driven by the same luminance change. Varying the luminance delivered to center vs. surround changes the relative mix of excitatory (bipolar) or inhibitory (amacrine) drive to a central ganglion cell. For example, in an ON ganglion cell, a bright spot placed in the surround activates amacrine cell inhibition and antagonizes the excitatory effects of a center spot that drives bipolar cells. A bright spot placed in the surround of this neuron evokes only amacrine inhibition and does not drive spikes. A dark spot placed in the surround inhibits the amacrine cell and reduces inhibition to the ganglion cell; such disinhibition can lead ganglion cells to spike.

Bob Rodieck modelled excitatory (center) and inhibitory (surround) portions of the receptive field with two Gaussians, which offered great power in predicting ganglion cell responses to light62,63. It also showed that a given ganglion cell encodes an edge (or small spot), and not a homogenous patch of visual space. Rodieck’s models also made predictions about how center-surround organization could be used by downstream brain regions. For example, if a neuron in such a region has access to multiple center-surround receptive fields from several neighbouring ganglion cells, the precise location of a visual stimulus or pattern could be easily decoded. The most common cell types in the human retina, ON and OFF midget and ON and OFF parasol cells (which account for ~80% of ganglion cells), all show center-surround receptive fields64.

6.1.2 Colour opponency: First described in horizontal cells of fish65, colour opponency arises when a cell receives competing signals generated by visual stimuli of different wavelengths. In the primate retina, at the level of ganglion cells, it is generally believed that colour opponency is split along red-green66 and blue-yellow67 axes. Red-green opponency is thought to be computed in midget ganglion cells, some of which exhibit a biased spectral sampling within their receptive field centers to either red or green cones68. In contrast, blue-yellow opponency is thought to be computed by small bistratified ganglion cells67, as well as type of widefield ganglion cell69. While the exact role of colour opponency in the retina remains an area of active research70, signals from colour opponent ganglion cell types appear to make their way to colour-coding visual regions in the brain, which eventually lead to colour perception71.

Full width section: Colour Blindness. One of the first written accounts of colour blindness comes from John Dalton, of atomic theory fame, at the end of the 18th century. He noted that he and his brother were colour blind, and he assembled a small list of other colour blind people. Most happened to be males. Dalton hypothesized that his vitreous humour was tinted blue, and thus absorbed longer wavelengths72. He donated his eyes upon his death, but no pigment abnormalities were found with his vitreous humour nor his lens. It is now known that colour vision starts with the 3 different types of cones (blue, green and red, as outlined above). It turns out that about 8% of males are red-green colour blind, due to a genetic mutation leading to dysfunction of either red or green cones. Other types of colour blindness exist, but are more rare. In the 1990s, genetic analysis of Dalton’s preserved eye tissue revealed that he was missing functional middle wavelength (green) cones72.  [STD6] 

6.1.3 Direction selectivity: In the human and non-human primate visual system, it was generally thought that direction selective responses first arise in primary visual cortex. However, recent research has described direction selectivity in the primate retina73,74. This puts the primate retina in line with several animal models commonly studied, such as rabbits, mice and fish, where direction selectivity (DS) arises in retinal ganglion cells75, and where the circuitry underlying the DS computation is among the most studied in neuroscience. Early ideas came out of studies in insects and proposed that visual inputs from two spatially-offset locations were relayed to a single downstream neuron with differing delays76. A moving object that activated the long-delay input before the short delay input would drive neurons better since the delay allowed excitation from both locations to coincide. Motion in the opposite direction led to little coincidence, and so the neuron would respond selectively to motion direction. In contrast, retinal direction selectivity in mammals arises in ganglion cells primarily through direction-selective GABAergic inhibition from starburst amacrine cells75. These amacrine cells grow dendrites radially outwards from their cell bodies77. Starburst amacrine cells are preferentially activated by visual stimuli moving centripetally along their dendritic tree (i.e. moving away from the soma)78. The distal tip of each starburst dendrite connects with a ganglion cell. This ganglion cell in turn has the opposite preferred direction to that of its presynaptic starburst partner, since the amacrine cell releases GABA when activated79. In a remarkable feat of wiring, ganglion cells preferring a particular direction of motion receive inhibition exclusively from starburst amacrine cells dendrites that point the opposite (null) direction80. As such, stimuli moving in a ganglion cell’s preferred direction drive very little inhibition, whereas stimuli moving in the ganglion cell’s null direction drive strong GABAergic inhibition, which vetoes the relatively symmetrical excitatory drive from bipolar cells during both preferred and null direction motion. 

At least in the mouse and rabbit retina, there are several types of direction selective ganglion cells17. The best described are ON-OFF and ON DS cells81,82. The ON-OFF DS cells prefer relatively fast motion, and comprise types encoding motion along the cardinal axes (up, down, left and right). These cells send signals to the lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of the thalamus[LLM7]  and onto visual cortex83 (and also to the superior colliculus), but their role in visual perception is still unclear. ON DS cells prefer relatively slow motion and encode 3 directions that align with the axes of head motion encoded by the semicircular canals of the inner ear17,82. These cells project directly to accessory optic nuclei and are important for stabilizing eye movements, and help drive the vestibulo-ocular and optokinetic reflexes. 

6.1.4 Object motion sensitivity: An important task of the visual system is to separate object from background. An early correlate of this computation is thought to arise in the retina, with some ganglion cells preferentially responding when a small stimulus located in the receptive field center (i.e. the ‘object’) moves asynchronously from the stimulus impinging in its surround (i.e. the ‘background’)84. These cells have been described as “fly detectors”85, “local edge detectors86” and “object motion sensitive” cells87. Their properties arise because center excitation gets cancelled out by surround inhibition during synchronous center-surround motion84,87–89. Ensuring that surround inhibition is able to cancel out center excitation during such synchronous motion requires special circuitry, since signals in the center have to only travel through a bipolar cell to reach the ganglion cell, whereas surround motion signals must travel through a bipolar cell and an amacrine cell. Center bipolar excitation is slowed down by several mechanisms including interactions with glutamatergic amacrine cells and intrinsic properties. Surround inhibition is sped up by using an amacrine cell that can fire action potentials. The effect is that this ganglion cell preferentially responds when the stimulus in its center moves asynchronously from the stimulus in its surround, which has been suggested to be a method of computing object motion.

6.2 Rod vs. cone signals take different paths to get to ganglion cells

Rod and cone mediated visual signals take different anatomical routes to arrive at retinal ganglion cells. Cones contact ON and OFF cone bipolar cells, which then directly contact ON and OFF ganglion cells respectively7. In contrast, rods predominantly contact rod bipolar cells (which are a type of ON bipolar cell)6. However, rod bipolar cells do not form synapses with ganglion cells90,91. Instead, rod bipolar cells make glutamatergic synapses with AII (pronounced A-2) amacrine cells6,91,92. AII amacrine cells pass rod signals to ON bipolars via electrical synapses, and OFF bipolars via glycinergic synapses34. Additionally, some rod responses get into the cone system via electrical coupling between rods and cones34, and some rods appear to directly synapse with OFF bipolar cells. 

6.3 Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells

Classically, the retina was considered to have 2 types of photoreceptors: rod and cones. However, around the turn of the 21st century, converging lines of evidence revealed that there was a population of ganglion cells that were themselves photosensitive93,94. Termed intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs), these cells contain melanopsin and can convert light into spiking responses in the absence of inputs from rod and cone circuits. ipRGCs project to several ‘non-image forming’ visual centers in the brain, where their inputs are important for controlling circadian rhythm and the pupillary light reflex31,93,94. Interestingly, ipRGCs generally also receive light-evoked inputs from rod and cone pathways, and thus represent a hybrid visual signalling channel.

6.4 Retinal output

While the axons of all retinal ganglion cells get bundled together and leave the eye via the optic nerve, each type of ganglion cells projects its axons to specific target regions in the brain. The most common retino-recipient targets are the LGN (which in turn projects to visual cortex which is responsible for conscious visual perception) and the superior colliculus (which is integral for controlling eye movements and integrating vision with other sensory inputs). Intrinsically photosensitive ganglion cells project to a host of other brain regions. In mice recent work has found approximately 45 different retino-recipient regions95.

  1. Retinal vision in the ‘real world’

 

Most of the physiological findings described above have been studied in ex vivo retinae (i.e. a retina in a dish). The real world introduces several additional challenges.

7.1 Eye movements move the visual image projected onto the retina. Aside from vergence eye movements described in Section 1, we make several distinct forms of eye movements, whose main roles are to focus our high-acuity foveal vision on regions of interest in the environment. To look around, we make saccadic eye movements — quick ballistic movements of the eye that can span either very small or very large trajectories28,29. Much of our visual perception appears to be supressed during saccades96–99. Next, as we move through the world we constantly move our head, and there are complex reflexes that stabilize our gaze during such movements. Finally, even when we fix our gaze on something, our eyes are never truly stable. Instead, our eyes exhibit microsaccades (and fixational drift)100, which help ensure that the image projected onto our retina is constantly updated101 and increase visual acuity102.

 

7.2 Light adaptation allows the retina to function across a wide range of light intensities. As outlined in the phototransduction section, photoreceptors encode only a limited range of luminance change about a mean in their membrane potential. At the level of ganglion cells, this means that a given ganglion cell’s firing rate is not an absolute measure of light intensity. Instead, firing rate represents a relative increase or decrease from the mean luminance. In fact, a given ganglion cell is only able to reliably signal increases in a spot’s intensity over about two orders of magnitude. However, through various mechanisms, the retina as a whole is able to adapt to the mean luminance of a given environment, meaning that the luminance response curve of a ganglion cell can shift depending on mean luminance, allowing us to see over roughly 10 orders of magnitude. However, light adaptation is not instantaneous, and can account for why it might take a minute for your retina to adjust when you pass between two environments with significantly different mean luminance levels (like walking from a dark room into a sunny environment).[STD9] 

 

  1. Retinal development

Darwin famously wrote that the complexity of the eye was almost enough to make one question the theory of natural selection: “To suppose that the eye with all its inimitable contrivances for adjusting the focus to different distances, for admitting different amounts of light, and for the correction of spherical and chromatic aberration, could have been formed by natural selection, seems, I freely confess, absurd”103. In recent years, scientists have been able to watch the retina develop in living organisms with incredible detail, providing critical insights into how such an “inimitable” structure is formed. The retina begins as the optic vesicle, which buds off the embryonic diencephalon. A series of events take place whereby this nascent eye generates an eye-cup, lens, blood vessels, and retina. The neural retina represents a part of the CNS that is conveniently located outside the skull, making it “an approachable part of the brain” that has proven to be a powerful model for the field of developmental biology.

 

8.1 Each retinal class is born at specific times and wiring is genetically defined. In rodents, a group of neuroblasts compose a sheet at the back surface of the growing eye cup. These cells have an I-beam shape when viewed in cross section, with anchors at the outer and inner margins of the developing retina. The nuclei within these cells undergo interkinetic migration, shuttling between the inner and outer margins of the retina, during which time they also generate daughter cells that will go on to become the various retinal classes. Retinal ganglion cells and amacrine cells are born first, bipolar cells next, and photoreceptors last. Current evidence indicates that within a neuron class, each subtype is generated randomly — for instance, neuroblasts generating only direction selective ganglion cells have not been found104. The generation of different neuronal classes happens over a range of days, and newly generated neurons must migrate to their final positions. The temporal delay in generation of different neuronal classes complicates the retinal wiring process, as the dendrites and axons of synaptic partners arrive asynchronously in the plexiform layers. To solve this problem, the retina relies on a stereotyped set of genetically defined adhesive cues that guide retinal circuit assembly105.

8.2 Spontaneous waves of activity pattern the neonatal retina and influence wiring in the brain. Both genetics and activity drive the development of the visual system. The sheet-like geometry of the retina permitted scientists to simultaneously record from many ganglion cells at once using an array of electrodes arranged on a glass plate106. The initial recordings from neonatal retinae — led by Rachel Wong and Markus Meister, working in the labs of Carla Shatz and Dennis Baylor — revealed a remarkable result: in the absence of visual stimulation, and even before the retina itself was fully wired together, spontaneous waves of activity spread across the retina. A host of follow-up studies showed that the mechanisms driving these waves change over the course of development107–109. Importantly, the waves are temporally local phenomena: the activity of a given ganglion cell is only closely correlated with that of its nearest neighbors, but not with distantly located ganglion cells in the same retina or ganglion cells from the other eye. These waves of retinal activity propagate to retino-recipient areas in the brain, where this patterned activity is important for the development of retinotopic maps. Pharmacologically or genetically disrupting wave activity has been shown to inhibit the development of proper retinotopic maps in higher brain areas. 

            

  1. Diseases, disorders, and treatments

It is estimated that over 2 billion people suffer from some form of visual impairment. Vision loss and impairment come in many forms. The source of the problem dictates the type of vision loss or impairment. The most common forms of visual impairment arise from lack of access to relatively simple procedures. The World Health Organization estimates that over 100 million people suffer from uncorrected refractive errors, over 60 million suffer from cataracts, and over 800 million suffer from unaddressed presbyopia. Here, we outlined the major forms of eye and retina-based visual impairments, and highlight emerging therapeutic strategies.

9.1 Cataracts

A cataract arises when the lens becomes cloudy3. Cataracts occur relatively commonly as humans age, though they can also have genetic or lifestyle-related causes (e.g. smoking, extended sun exposure, etc.). The extent of visual impairment caused by cataracts is wide ranging, from mild to completely obstructing the ability to focus images on the retina. Left untreated, it is a progressive issue. Cataracts can also arise in children, and if left untreated for extended periods of time (especially if present throughout the critical period), can affect vision even once removed. The first cataract surgical procedures were documented by Sushruta in India around 600 BCE (in which the lens was pushed out of the way, so as not affect incoming light), and the first documented cataract removal procedure was performed by Ammar, working in Egypt around 1000 CE. Currently, the standard protocol is to replace the cataract with an artificial lens, or simply remove the lens. Both approaches eliminate accommodation, but artificial lenses can be multifocal, to help account for this.

9.2 Glaucoma

Glaucoma is a leading age-related disease that affects the health of retinal ganglion cells and the optic nerve3. Increased intra-ocular pressure is thought to be a leading culprit behind glaucoma, but much remains to be known about this disease. Glaucoma tends to develop slowly over time, though some forms are fast onset. In general, glaucoma correlates with old age. To date, there are limited treatment strategies for glaucoma other than medications to reduce intraocular pressure, which are usually delivered in the form of eye drops.

9.3 Diabetic retinopathy

A majority of people suffering from diabetes for over 20 years will experience diabetic retinopathy3. It is caused by excess vasculature growth, and leaky or bursting vasculature which leads to edema, both of which can lead to retinal damage. Common treatments include laser surgery, injection of corticosteroids into the eye, or delivery of anti-VEGF compounds into the eye.

9.4 Age-related macular degeneration

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is an adult onset disease in which the central part of the visual field — the macula, including the fovea and its immediate surroundings — is affected3. This results in damage to photoreceptors (specifically cones which are dominant in the macula), leading to a blind spot (scotoma) in the high acuity central part of the visual field. Aside from age-related macular degeneration, there are also juvenile onset forms of macular degeneration, such as Stargardt’s disease. As macular degeneration progresses, it can eventually lead to significant vision loss outside of the macula. AMD comes in two forms: wet and dry. Dry AMD is by far the most common, and results form drusen (protein and lipid aggregates) building up between the retina pigment epithelium and choroid. There are currently limited treatment strategies for dry AMD. Wet AMD is neovascular and can be treated with anti-VEGF agents.

9.5 Retinitis pigmentosa 

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) is a group of diverse genetic diseases that lead to loss of rod photoreceptors3. Since rods are dominant in the peripheral retina (outside the fovea/macula), retinitis pigmentosa leads to ‘tunnel vision’ and night-blindness. As the disease progresses, it can begin to impinge upon the macula due to secondary cone loss, resulting in additional loss of central vision. Currently, there are limited strategies for treating retinitis pigmentosa.

9.6 Treatment strategies for retinal-based vision loss

9.6.1 Gene therapy is a therapeutic approach in which a mutated gene is replaced with the correct copy. Diseases caused by single gene mutations, in which the mutation does not lead to cell death or other deleterious effects, are the central target of gene therapy approaches. To date, the standard protocol is to package the proper version of the gene of interest into an adeno-associated virus, and inject the virus into or nearby the affected tissue of interest. For instance, the first ever gene therapy approved for use in humans was Luxturna, targeted towards treating a form of Leber’s congenital amaurosis, by providing the correct version of the gene RPE65.


 9.6.2 Cell therapy involves using stem cells to replace dead or dystrophic retinal neurons. For instance, in a dish, stem cells or re-derived stem cells can be converted to photoreceptors. These photoreceptors can then be injected into the retina where the hope is that they will survive and form normal connections within the retina, thus restoring light sensitivity. Another approach could be to grow full retina in a dish, as a retinal organoid, and then harvest cells of interest from the organoid.

9.6.3 Retinal prostheses are electronic chips that are implanted in the eye, in direct contact with the retina. The chips contain multiple electrodes, each of which can electrically stimulate local regions of the retina, with such stimulation resulting in perceptual ‘phosphenes’ — somewhat structure-less luminance perceptions — within the electrically stimulated portion of the visual field. Retinal prosthetic implants are combined with head-worn goggles that record the visual scene being viewed by the subject via a camera, and this video signal is used to control which electrodes are stimulated at each time point. Another version of this technique that could be used when the retina is not an appropriate therapeutic target, such as in late-stage glaucoma, would be to place the stimulating electrodes in visual cortex.

9.6.4 Optogenetic retinal therapy is a form a gene therapy that relates closely to the retinal prosthetic approach. For this method, an adeno-associated virus is injected into the eye to drive expression of an optogenetic opsin into retinal neurons (e.g. bipolar cells, ganglion cells) that remain following photoreceptor degeneration in diseases such as macular degeneration and retinitis pigmentosa. 

  1. Looking forward

 

It would be remiss if we failed to include a visual pun in the chapter, or a retinal drawing from Cajal, or if we didn’t conclude by emphasizing how much knowledge has been acquired about the eye and retina over the last 100 years or so (as outlined above). Nonetheless, in this final section, we will focus on areas that remain poorly understood, and where significant gains in knowledge remain possible.

Much of the chapter has focused on neuron classes, and within each class, cell types. Much work on cell types has been restricted to studies in mouse retina, so work over the coming years will need to estend this work to the non-human primate and human retina. For example, what visual features are encoded by the human retinal ganglion cell types that have not been well studied (i.e. all cell types other than midget, parasol and small bistratified)? Furthermore, what behavioral relevance do these different cell types serve, and which types, if any, are most susceptible to dystrophy or most fundamental to be targeted for vision restoration?

Furthermore, while many of the breakthroughs in retinal research in recent years have come from studies in mouse retina, mice lack a fovea, which is a crucial part of the human visual system. In the coming years, increased studies in foveated animal models will be required to examine difference in cell types and circuit computations in foveal vs. peripheral retina, in order to better understand our visual system and develop therapeutics targeted toward disorders that affect central vs. peripheral vision.

Finally, we are at turning point where many different strategies for restoring sight to the visually-impaired retina are nearing clinical use. These are still early days, but soon many patients will be receiving some restored visual capabilities. This is remarkable, but we must keep in mind that often this restored vision will be highly attenuated, or in other ways quite different than normal retinal vision. We must therefore strive to understand the extent to which we can (or need to) restore normal retinal vision, and how various forms of restoration lead to different degrees of visual competency and patient satisfaction.
